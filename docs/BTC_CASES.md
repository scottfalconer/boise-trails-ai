# BTC Heuristic Cases

Cases are concrete observed or contrastive examples that explain why a heuristic exists. Keep them public-safe and specific enough that a future agent can recognize the pattern.

Do not include raw private GPS traces, exact home-origin data, tokens, private dashboard data, raw BTC dashboard payloads, or participant-heavy leaderboard/history payloads.

## Failure-Mode Cases

| ID | Failure mode | Symptom | Why it fails | Mitigation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `btc_failure_access_001` | Mapped trailhead treated as guaranteed access | The assistant recommends starting at a minor mapped trailhead without checking parking, gates, road passability, signage, or legal access. | BTC routes can be graph-valid but human-invalid if the start point cannot be legally or practically used. | Run the BTC Trailhead Affordance Check. |
| `btc_failure_access_002` | Nearest-road tunnel vision | The assistant treats an uncertain residential road pin as the only possible access answer and rejects the route when that pin cannot be certified. | A slightly farther park or formal trailhead can be the better optimization because it preserves legality and cueability while still saving miles/time versus baseline. | Search outward for certifiable parking and rerun runnable-cost math with connector mileage included. |
| `btc_failure_legality_001` | Published trail-management rule ignored | A route is certified because official BTC segment coverage and ascent direction pass, even though Ridge to Rivers all-user direction rules make the GPX illegal or socially unsafe. | Official challenge data is not the whole field-readiness contract; land-manager rules govern whether the route can be run. | Run the special-management rule audit and block violations such as `special_management_direction_violated`. |
| `btc_failure_edge_001` | Required trail segments treated like waypoints | The assistant optimizes a route to visit trailheads or trail names instead of covering official segment geometry. | Challenge credit depends on full official segment-edge coverage, not visiting points or names. | Run the BTC Edge Coverage Audit. |
| `btc_failure_graph_002` | One-car route decomposed into phase excursions | A route card returns to the parked car or trailhead before clearing all required edges, then resumes another required segment cluster as if it were a separate outing. | The route may be credit-valid but wastes field movement and violates the closed edge-cover shape for a one-car card. | Treat the trailhead as depot-only, rebuild the route as one required-edge tour, and allow spur out-and-backs when topology requires them. |
| `btc_failure_artifact_001` | User-facing artifacts disagree about the route | The map, written menu, phone packet, and GPX show different mileages or route order. | Field readiness requires one canonical route truth across source, GPX, cues, and map. | Fix the canonical source or exporter and regenerate artifacts together. |
| `btc_failure_artifact_002` | Certified replacement not preserved | The exact-credit route-review gate passes, but the active packet restores stale Harlow / Avimor split cards that H1 certification said were removed. | Route-local review can pass while the source/checkpoint boundary is contradictory; the runner sees stale route truth. | Compare the active packet to certification checkpoints and accepted replacement manifests, then either regenerate from the replacement source or explicitly supersede the old checkpoint. |
| `btc_failure_artifact_003` | Unsupported ownership promotion deletes source card | A promotion row says a route now owns Quick Draw, Highlands, or Shane's credit, but the current audit evidence does not show the promoted owner route earning that segment cleanly. | The source card disappears even though the remaining card is not a human-valid or evidence-backed owner of the official segment. | Fail the promotion evidence gate, mark the row `retracted`, restore the source card, and recertify the packet. |
| `btc_failure_artifact_004` | Segment ids match but route metrics drift | A route has the same official segment ids in the canonical menu and phone packet, but on-foot miles, p75, live-map mileage, or connector-savings metadata differ. | Segment accounting can pass while the field-facing map and card give contradictory operational information. | Add a canonical route metric comparison and stale cue-metadata proof gate; regenerate every artifact from one source truth before field use. |
| `btc_failure_field_002` | Cue anchor crosses parked-car pass | A phone cue claims a short leg, but the active GPX/map leg spans several extra miles and crosses the parked car before the next cue. | The map can be continuous while the field instruction is incoherent; a runner cannot trust cue mileage or next-action context. | Mark `blocked_navigation_source`, repair/split the canonical source, and recertify the route card and field-day layer. |
| `btc_failure_field_005` | Natural loop split into ownership buckets | A route card repeats the wrong side of a loop because one package owns a named trail while another owns the obvious return trail, even though a single car-to-car loop would earn both. | Segment accounting passes while the field route is harder to understand and wastes or misassigns movement. | Recombine the official edges into the human-recognizable loop or hold the route; do not preserve package ownership if it contradicts the field route. |
| `btc_failure_field_004` | Live map skips the start cue | The live map defaults to cue 2 because cue 1 is a zero-length start marker and cue 2 begins at the same route-mile anchor. | In the field, the first screen hides the actual start instruction and makes the runner begin from a later leg. | Make the live-map startup invariant executable: initial active cue must be cue 1 unless the generator removes/merges the zero-length cue as non-active marker context. |
| `btc_failure_progress_003` | Latent official credit left assigned elsewhere | A field-packet GPX completes official segments that are still scheduled as new credit on another active route card. | Each route can pass its own claimed coverage while the active menu double-counts future work and hides an optimization. | Run the field latent-credit audit and repair canonical route/source data before promotion. |
| `btc_failure_connector_001` | Credit-correct route keeps an unnecessary repeat | The assistant defends a repeat/out-and-back as needed for credit even after that credit/access purpose is already satisfied. | The route may satisfy segment accounting while wasting field time or making the cue sequence worse than a shorter legal connector. | Re-check connector routing, provenance, elevation/direction cost, and phone cue labels. Hard-fail if the connector graph proves a materially shorter legal path by actual replacement geometry that avoids the already-credited repeat. |
| `btc_failure_cost_002` | Certified multi-start split disappears after recalculation | After Harrison was marked complete, active recalculation collapsed `1A` back into one West Climb plus 36th Street Chute outing instead of preserving the accepted split/re-park analysis. | The planner treated the multi-start result as a remembered private override rather than a standing runnable-cost rule. | Run multi-start audit and generated replacement promotion inside the active recalculation pipeline, then certify the resulting field packet. |
| `btc_failure_schedule_001` | Weekday/weekend capacity proxy | A promotion review says a 324-minute route is acceptable because it fits a weekend bound but is too close for a weekday profile. | For this user, weekday versus weekend is not a reliable capacity prior; the actual dated window can be equal or better during the week. | Use explicit date-specific availability and hard stops for placement and promotion gates. |
| `btc_failure_exception_001` | Route-specific fix hides a general rule | A Harrison-specific exporter or audit branch protects one known route, but the same access, overlap, or accepted-replacement pattern could fail on another outing. | The code path fixes the symptom without teaching the planner/exporter/audit the reusable rule. | Keep the guard temporarily, log it as exception debt, and replace it with geometry/access/certification-driven logic or data-backed local reality. |
| `btc_failure_field_day_001` | Calendar proof is mistaken for field guide | A relaxed-drive schedule covers every official segment, but the selected loops are not all promoted route cards and there is no day-level handoff showing route-card GPX, transfer cost, or publication gaps. | The runner has a mathematical schedule, not a field-executable day plan; a loop can be valid in an optimizer while still lacking route-card certification or day-level GPX validation. | Generate a field-day layer over certified route cards, flag unmatched loops as promotion gaps, and validate multi-loop day GPX before publication. |
| `btc_failure_field_day_002` | Full inventory audit masks selected-day blockers | After the field-day layer exists, the full field-tool audit still fails on unselected route cards, while the selected layer has four cue/card blockers and two day-level GPX handoff gaps that directly affect runnable dates. | Cleaning every route-card failure is useful, but it is the wrong first queue for deciding what can run during the challenge window. | Start with selected field-day loop blockers, then day-level handoff validation, then promotion gaps ranked by selected-day pressure, with full-inventory cleanup as backlog. |
| `btc_failure_review_001` | Certified-but-dominated route | FD14D has coherent route artifacts for segment `1482`, but the selected Full Sail start adds avoidable access/return burden compared with an accepted lower 36th start for the same credit. | GPX/cue certification answers "can this be run?", not "is this the right start for the credit target?" | Block promotion with `FAIL_DOMINATED` unless the route is regenerated from the accepted anchor or a current route/source-hashed waiver documents why the shorter anchor is invalid. |

### 2026-05-08: Harrison Hollow Full Rerun

Source log: `years/2026/field-tests/pre-challenge/2026-05-08-test-03/`

Field-test result:

- Planned outing: `1B. Harrison Hollow`.
- Planned card: 141 min p75 / 158 min p90 door-to-door, 6.36 on-foot miles, 4.72 official miles, 12 planned official segments.
- Actual door-to-door time: 2:11:06.34, about 131.1 minutes.
- Strava reconstruction: 6.46 mi, 1:41:15 moving time, 1:58:52 elapsed recording time, 1,186 ft gain.
- Local matcher result: 12/12 planned official segments matched, 4.72/4.72 planned official miles matched.
- Extra matched segment: `Buena Vista Trail 5` / segment `1755`, 0.14 official mi.
- Scope: pre-challenge planning evidence only; do not count as official 2026 BTC progress.

Daily improvements created:

- Keep `1B. Harrison Hollow` calibrated as a roughly 2h10-2h20 field outing; do not lower p75 from one clean sample.
- Treat `Fit GPS` as a cue-leg operation: fit the current GPS point plus the next cue, not the whole route.
- Make `Next cue` advance to the next distinct field leg, not through same-location segment start/end duplicates.
- Use a multi-color progress ribbon so distance along route is visually legible at a glance.
- Detect same-corridor overlap generically and surface active-leg / double-back warnings rather than relying on route-specific prose.
- Expose cue-level elevation asymmetry, especially where a route is reasonable downhill but would be a poor uphill choice.
- Record the connector-repeat failure mode: once credit/access purpose is satisfied, non-credit repeat movement must be re-optimized as the shortest legal/elevation-aware connector to the next cue.
- If an active GPX line uses connector or already-credited official mileage inside an official cue, the phone cue must say that explicitly; the runner should not have to infer it from the basemap.

Reusable heuristic case:

The Harrison route was credit-correct and ultimately field-successful, but the map also showed `#53 Buena Vista` in both directions around the `#52` / `#50` transition. That revealed a separate route-quality failure: the planner preserved a repeat/out-and-back from the segment-order chain after its purpose was satisfied. Future agents should distinguish "needed to earn credit" from "still the best legal connector after credit is already handled," then compare legal alternatives with elevation/direction cost before defending the repeat.
